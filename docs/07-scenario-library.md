# VALOUR Scenario Library

Version: 0.1
Status: Private MVP scenario library

## Purpose

This document defines the first reusable scenario library for VALOUR for Architecture Leadership.

The scenario library is the heart of the first MVP. Each scenario should help a user prepare for, rehearse, and review a real leadership moment.

## Scenario design pattern

Each scenario should follow the same structure:

1. Situation
2. User risk
3. Stakeholder risk
4. Desired outcome
5. Preparation questions
6. Rehearsal prompt
7. Better language patterns
8. After-action review
9. Improvement focus

## Scenario 1: Architecture review board

### Situation

The user needs to present an architecture decision, risk, design, exemption, or recommendation to a review board.

### User risk

The user may over-explain, become defensive, go too technical, or fail to make the decision clear.

### Stakeholder risk

The board may see the proposal as unclear, risky, incomplete, or not aligned to standards.

### Desired outcome

The board understands the recommendation, trade-offs, decision required, risks, and next steps.

### Preparation questions

What decision is required?

What is the simplest version of the recommendation?

What are the main options?

What is the trade-off?

What risk needs explicit acceptance?

What is likely to be challenged?

### Rehearsal prompt

Act as an architecture review board. Challenge my proposal on clarity, risk, standards, business alignment, implementation feasibility, and decision quality. Ask one question at a time. After each answer, give feedback on whether I was clear, concise, and credible.

### Better language patterns

The decision required today is...

The recommendation is...

The trade-off is...

The main risk is...

The reason this is acceptable is...

The next step is...

### After-action review

Did I state the decision clearly?

Did I stay at the right altitude?

Did I answer challenge without becoming defensive?

Did the board understand the trade-offs?

What would I shorten next time?

## Scenario 2: Executive briefing

### Situation

The user needs to brief senior stakeholders on a complex technology decision.

### User risk

The user may provide too much technical detail, fail to connect to business impact, or miss the decision the executive needs to make.

### Desired outcome

The executive understands the business implication, recommendation, options, cost or risk, and decision required.

### Preparation questions

What does the executive care about?

What is the business impact?

What decision do they need to make?

What information is unnecessary at this level?

What question are they most likely to ask?

### Rehearsal prompt

Act as a time-poor executive. I have five minutes. Ask me to explain the recommendation, business impact, cost, risk, and decision required. Push back if I become too technical or unclear.

### Better language patterns

The business issue is...

The decision is...

The technology implication is...

The risk of doing nothing is...

The recommended option is...

The executive summary is...

### After-action review

Did I respect the executive's time?

Did I translate technical detail into business language?

Did I answer directly?

Did I make the decision clear?

## Scenario 3: Discovery workshop

### Situation

The user needs to run a workshop with stakeholders to understand goals, constraints, processes, problems, and decision criteria.

### User risk

The user may talk too much, jump to solutions too early, or lose control of the session.

### Desired outcome

The workshop produces clear inputs, decisions, constraints, risks, and follow-up actions.

### Preparation questions

What must be learned?

Who is in the room?

What do they each care about?

What must not be solved too early?

What decisions are required after the session?

### Rehearsal prompt

Act as a mixed stakeholder group in a discovery workshop. Include unclear requirements, conflicting priorities, and one dominant participant. Help me practise facilitation, clarification, and control of the room.

### Better language patterns

Let me pause us there.

What problem are we solving first?

What is the decision criteria?

Who owns that process today?

What would make this fail?

I want to separate the requirement from the solution.

### After-action review

Did I ask more than I told?

Did I manage the dominant voices?

Did I capture decisions and unknowns?

Did I avoid premature solutioning?

## Scenario 4: Challenging a poor solution

### Situation

The user believes a proposed solution is weak, risky, misaligned, or poorly designed.

### User risk

The user may sound dismissive, arrogant, obstructive, or overly negative.

### Desired outcome

The concern is understood without damaging trust, and the group moves toward a better option.

### Preparation questions

What is wrong with the solution?

What evidence supports the concern?

What is the impact if ignored?

What alternative is available?

How can the challenge preserve dignity?

### Rehearsal prompt

Act as a defensive delivery lead who believes the current solution is good enough. Help me practise challenging the solution without sounding arrogant or obstructive.

### Better language patterns

I can see why that option is attractive.

The concern I have is...

The risk shows up when...

A better option may be...

Can we test this against the decision criteria?

I am not trying to block the path; I am trying to reduce rework later.

### After-action review

Did I challenge the solution or the person?

Did I offer a constructive alternative?

Did I stay respectful?

Did I make the risk concrete?

## Scenario 5: Security or risk pushback

### Situation

Security, risk, compliance, or governance stakeholders challenge the architecture approach.

### User risk

The user may become defensive, dismissive, or overly technical.

### Desired outcome

The user clarifies the concern, aligns on risk treatment, and identifies a practical path forward.

### Preparation questions

What control or risk is being challenged?

Is the concern valid?

What evidence do I have?

What mitigation is available?

What decision is needed?

### Rehearsal prompt

Act as a security or risk stakeholder challenging my architecture. Ask direct questions about controls, residual risk, auditability, and accountability. Give feedback on whether my response builds trust.

### Better language patterns

That is a valid concern.

Let me separate the control requirement from the implementation option.

The residual risk is...

The proposed mitigation is...

The decision needed is...

We should document that assumption.

### After-action review

Did I listen before defending?

Did I clarify the risk?

Did I produce a practical next step?

Did I preserve trust with risk stakeholders?

## Scenario 6: Delivery conflict

### Situation

The user is in conflict with a project manager, delivery lead, vendor, or implementation team.

### User risk

The user may become frustrated, be seen as slowing delivery, or fail to separate architecture concern from delivery pressure.

### Desired outcome

The group agrees on the issue, impact, decision, and next action.

### Preparation questions

What is the delivery pressure?

What is the architecture concern?

What is the consequence of ignoring it?

What can be compromised?

What cannot be compromised?

### Rehearsal prompt

Act as a delivery lead under time pressure. Challenge me on why architecture is slowing the project. Help me practise staying calm, practical, and outcome-focused.

### Better language patterns

I understand the delivery pressure.

The reason I am raising this now is...

Here is what can move forward.

Here is what needs a decision.

Here is the minimum architecture position I can support.

### After-action review

Did I acknowledge delivery pressure?

Did I avoid sounding bureaucratic?

Did I identify a practical path?

Did I protect the architecture concern?

## Scenario 7: Architecture trade-off explanation

### Situation

The user needs to explain options and trade-offs to stakeholders.

### User risk

The user may present too many options, fail to recommend, or overwhelm the audience.

### Desired outcome

Stakeholders understand the options, trade-offs, recommendation, and decision criteria.

### Preparation questions

What are the realistic options?

What criteria matter?

What is the recommendation?

What trade-off must be accepted?

What is the simplest decision frame?

### Rehearsal prompt

Act as a mixed business and technology audience. Ask me to explain three options and recommend one. Push back if I fail to make the trade-off clear.

### Better language patterns

There are three realistic options.

Option one optimises for...

Option two reduces...

Option three increases...

The recommendation is...

The trade-off we must accept is...

### After-action review

Did I recommend clearly?

Did I explain trade-offs without drowning the room?

Did I connect to decision criteria?

## Scenario 8: Strategy-to-architecture translation

### Situation

The user needs to convert business strategy into architecture options and next steps.

### User risk

The user may stay too abstract or jump too quickly into technology.

### Desired outcome

The strategy is translated into capabilities, constraints, architecture options, and execution steps.

### Preparation questions

What is the strategy trying to achieve?

What capabilities are required?

What constraints exist?

What architecture decisions are implied?

What sequence makes sense?

### Rehearsal prompt

Act as a business sponsor with a strategic goal but limited technical detail. Help me practise translating strategy into architecture without overcomplicating it.

### Better language patterns

If that is the strategy, the capability implication is...

The architecture question becomes...

The first decision is...

The constraint we need to test is...

The next step is...

### After-action review

Did I connect strategy to capability?

Did I avoid technology too early?

Did I create a useful decision path?

## Scenario 9: First meeting with a new team

### Situation

The user is entering a new team, program, client, or leadership context.

### User risk

The user may over-position, talk too much, appear detached, or fail to build trust.

### Desired outcome

The user establishes credibility, listens well, clarifies role, and builds trust.

### Preparation questions

What do they need to know about me?

What do I need to learn from them?

What should I not say too early?

What trust signal matters most?

What expectations need to be clarified?

### Rehearsal prompt

Act as a new team meeting me for the first time. Include cautious, sceptical, and disengaged participants. Help me practise opening the conversation with authority, humility, and clarity.

### Better language patterns

My role here is...

Before I give views, I want to understand...

What has worked so far?

Where are the current pain points?

How can architecture be useful rather than heavy?

### After-action review

Did I listen before leading?

Did I establish role clarity?

Did I avoid over-explaining?

Did I build trust?

## Scenario 10: End-of-day handover

### Situation

The user is closing professional mode and moving into personal life.

### User risk

The user may keep working mentally, carry frustration home, overbuild, isolate, or fail to recover.

### Desired outcome

The user closes work, captures open loops, and moves into VITA with a simple personal plan.

### Preparation questions

What remains open?

What can wait?

What emotional residue am I carrying?

What does my body need?

What personal action matters tonight?

### Rehearsal prompt

Act as VALLUM. Help me close the workday, identify what must be parked, and hand me over to VITA with a simple recovery plan.

### Better language patterns

Work is closed enough for today.

This issue is captured and does not need to be carried tonight.

The next professional action is...

The personal action now is...

### After-action review

Did I close the loop?

Did I carry work into the evening?

Did I follow the personal plan?

Did I recover enough to start clean tomorrow?

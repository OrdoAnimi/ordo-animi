# Business Architecture Brief

Version: 0.1
Status: Private business architecture brief
Phase: Architecture and Pilot Build

## Purpose

This document defines the business architecture for VALOUR for Architecture Leadership.

The business architecture explains the users, value streams, operating model, capabilities, commercial path, and pilot business outcomes.

## Business objective

Create a pilot-ready product that helps architects and technical leaders prepare for, rehearse, and review high-stakes architecture leadership conversations.

The first business objective is not scale. The first objective is proof that the VALOUR workflow creates enough value for users to continue, recommend, sponsor, or pay.

## Target users

Primary users:

- Enterprise architects
- Solution architects
- Cloud architects
- Security architects
- Domain architects
- Principal consultants
- Architecture practice leads
- Technical leaders moving into advisory roles

Secondary users:

- Delivery leaders
- Transformation leads
- Technology managers
- Consulting managers
- Architecture capability leads

## Buyer profiles

Potential buyers include:

- Architecture practice leads
- Heads of architecture
- CTO or CIO functions
- Consulting firm leaders
- Cloud partner leaders
- Transformation leaders
- Capability and learning leads

## Core value proposition

VALOUR helps architects become more trusted and effective in the conversations where architecture decisions are shaped.

It does this by helping them prepare, rehearse, refine language, apply learning in real situations, and review performance afterward.

## Business problem

Architecture teams often produce methods, artefacts, and recommendations, but may still struggle to influence decisions.

The gap is not only technical. It is conversational, behavioural, and leadership-based.

Common business impacts include:

- Weak stakeholder trust
- Poor decision clarity
- Rework from misunderstood trade-offs
- Architecture seen as a blocker
- Executives disengaged by technical detail
- Delivery teams frustrated by governance
- Strong technical staff not ready for leadership rooms

## Business value streams

### Value stream 1: Prepare the leader

The user brings a real scenario. VALOUR clarifies audience, outcome, risk, decision, and message.

### Value stream 2: Rehearse the moment

The user rehearses likely questions, resistance, challenge, or ambiguity before the real meeting.

### Value stream 3: Improve the language

VALOUR helps the user shorten, reframe, soften, strengthen, or clarify responses.

### Value stream 4: Apply in the real world

The user enters the actual conversation better prepared.

### Value stream 5: Review and learn

The user reflects on what happened and receives a pattern snapshot.

### Value stream 6: Build evidence

The pilot captures whether VALOUR improved confidence, clarity, usefulness, and repeat intent.

## Capabilities required

### Pilot management

Manage users, scenarios, records, and pilot status.

### Scenario guidance

Help users select and work through one scenario.

### AI rehearsal

Simulate or guide challenging conversations using structured prompts.

### Language refinement

Improve answers for clarity, brevity, empathy, authority, and decision framing.

### Pattern review

Identify repeated user patterns and development focus.

### Evidence capture

Measure usefulness, confidence shift, repeat intent, and willingness to continue.

### Commercial conversion

Convert pilot evidence into next-step offers, team pilots, funder materials, or product build decisions.

## Operating model

The first operating model is founder-led.

Roles:

- Founder/product lead: owns product direction and delivery.
- Pilot operator: runs the workflow and captures evidence.
- Pilot user: brings a real scenario and completes the loop.
- Advisor: reviews market, product, or architecture as needed.

In the first version, the founder may perform all operator roles.

## Pilot business process

1. Identify pilot user.
2. Confirm fit.
3. Complete intake.
4. Select scenario.
5. Prepare and rehearse.
6. User applies learning.
7. Complete after-action review.
8. Produce pattern report.
9. Capture evidence.
10. Decide next commercial action.

## Commercial pathway

Initial path:

- Founder-led pilot
- Individual paid pilot
- Small team pilot
- Architecture practice pilot
- Lightweight product build
- Partner or enterprise pathway

## Business architecture risks

### Risk: product too broad

Mitigation: keep first product focused on architecture leadership.

### Risk: weak buyer urgency

Mitigation: test with practice leads and leaders who own architecture capability.

### Risk: users like the idea but do not act

Mitigation: require a real scenario and real after-action review.

### Risk: too much manual work

Mitigation: document the manual process and automate only repeated steps after evidence exists.

### Risk: unclear commercial owner

Mitigation: track whether value is individual, team, practice, or enterprise level.

## Business architecture conclusion

VALOUR has a clear initial business architecture when it stays focused on architecture leadership.

The business model should begin with founder-led pilots and move toward repeatable team pilots before platform scale.
